Tuberculosis (TB) is a disease caused by bacteria that usually attack the lungs, but they can attack any part of the body such as the kidney, spine, and brain. If not treated properly, TB disease can be fatal.

Common symptoms include a bad cough that lasts 3 weeks or longer, pain in the chest, coughing up blood or sputum, weakness or fatigue, weight loss, no appetite, chills, fever, and sweating at night.

Risk factors include a weakened immune system, close contact with someone with infectious TB disease, and living or working in areas where TB is common.

Prevention involves identifying and treating latent TB infections, good infection control measures, and in some countries, the BCG vaccine.

Urgent medical care is needed if you experience a persistent cough, especially if you are coughing up blood, have chest pain, or experience unexplained weight loss and night sweats.
