Fever is a temporary increase in your body temperature, often due to an illness or infection. It is a sign that your body's immune system is actively fighting an invader, such as viruses, bacteria, fungi, or parasites. A normal body temperature is typically around 98.6°F (37°C), though a fever is usually defined as a temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher.

Common infections that cause fever include the common cold, influenza (flu), urinary tract infections (UTIs), stomach bugs (gastroenteritis), and respiratory infections like COVID-19 or pneumonia.

Symptoms accompanying a fever often depend on the underlying infection but can include sweating, chills and shivering, headache, muscle aches, loss of appetite, irritability, and general weakness.

Prevention of common infections involves practicing good hygiene. This includes washing hands frequently with soap and water, avoiding close contact with people who are sick, covering your mouth when coughing or sneezing, and staying up to date on recommended vaccinations. Management of a mild fever generally involves resting, drinking plenty of fluids to prevent dehydration, and taking over-the-counter fever reducers if uncomfortable.

Urgent medical care is needed if a fever reaches 103°F (39.4°C) or higher, lasts longer than a few days, or is accompanied by severe symptoms such as a stiff neck, confusion, difficulty breathing, a worsening rash, or seizures. Infants under 3 months old with any fever should be seen by a doctor immediately.
