Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a common, preventable, and treatable chronic lung disease that affects men and women worldwide. It causes restricted airflow and breathing problems. The most common conditions contributing to COPD are emphysema and chronic bronchitis.

The main symptoms include a persistent cough that may produce mucus (phlegm), shortness of breath (especially during physical activity), wheezing, and chest tightness. These symptoms typically worsen over time and can make daily activities difficult.

The primary cause and major risk factor for COPD is tobacco smoking. Other risk factors include long-term exposure to indoor and outdoor air pollution, occupational dusts and chemicals, and frequent lower respiratory infections during childhood.

Prevention and management are heavily focused on avoiding the main risk factors. Quitting smoking is the most effective way to prevent COPD or slow its progression. Management strategies may include using inhalers to open airways, pulmonary rehabilitation, avoiding air pollution, and getting vaccinated against flu and pneumonia to prevent exacerbations.

Urgent medical care is required during a severe exacerbation (flare-up) when breathing becomes extremely difficult, lips or fingernails turn blue (cyanosis), or if confusion or a rapid heartbeat occurs.
