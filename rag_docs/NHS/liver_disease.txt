Liver disease encompasses a wide range of conditions that damage the liver and prevent it from functioning properly. The liver is a vital organ responsible for digesting food, filtering toxins from the blood, and fighting infections. 

Common types of liver disease include hepatitis (viral infections like Hepatitis A, B, and C), fatty liver disease (both alcohol-related and non-alcohol-related), autoimmune liver diseases, and cirrhosis (severe scarring of the liver).

Symptoms of liver disease may not be noticeable until the damage is advanced. When symptoms do occur, they can include jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes), abdominal pain and swelling, chronic fatigue, nausea or vomiting, dark urine, and pale stool.

Major risk factors include heavy alcohol consumption, obesity, type 2 diabetes, sharing needles, unprotected sex, and exposure to certain chemicals or toxins.

Prevention focuses on maintaining a healthy lifestyle. This includes drinking alcohol in moderation or not at all, maintaining a healthy weight, getting vaccinated against Hepatitis A and B, practicing safe sex, and avoiding contact with other people's blood and body fluids.

Urgent medical care is needed if you experience severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, yellowing of the skin, or if you vomit blood or pass black, tarry stools, as these can be signs of liver failure or severe complications.
