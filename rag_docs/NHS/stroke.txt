A stroke is a serious, life-threatening medical condition that occurs when the blood supply to part of the brain is cut off. Because it starves brain cells of oxygen, causing them to swell and stop functioning, it is considered a medical emergency that requires urgent treatment.

There are two primary types of stroke:
- Ischaemic stroke: Caused by a blockage, typically a blood clot, in an artery leading to the brain.
- Haemorrhagic stroke: Occurs when there is bleeding in or around the brain, which disrupts normal blood flow.
A related condition is a transient ischaemic attack (TIA), often called a "mini-stroke." This is a temporary blockage of the arteries to the brain. TIAs are considered a warning sign of a potential future stroke and should be treated urgently.

Common symptoms can be remembered with the FAST test:
- Face: Has their face fallen on one side? Can they smile?
- Arms: Can they raise both arms and keep them there?
- Speech: Is their speech slurred?
- Time: Time to call emergency services if you see any of these signs.

Major risk factors include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, atrial fibrillation, and a family history of stroke.

Prevention involves adopting a healthy diet, exercising regularly, stopping smoking, and limiting alcohol intake. Managing underlying conditions like high blood pressure and diabetes is also critical.

Urgent medical care is absolutely necessary if a stroke is suspected. Dial emergency services immediately. Early treatment can significantly reduce brain damage and complications.
