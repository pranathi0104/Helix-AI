Chronic kidney disease (CKD) occurs when the kidneys are damaged and cannot filter blood properly, leading to a buildup of waste and fluids in the body. It is a significant health issue, and many people are unaware they have the condition because symptoms may not be obvious in the early stages.

The primary risk factors for developing CKD include diabetes, high blood pressure, heart disease, obesity, and a family history of kidney disease. High blood sugar from diabetes can damage the nephrons (tiny filters) in the kidneys over time. High blood pressure can also severely damage blood vessels in the kidneys.

Common symptoms in later stages may include fatigue, swelling in the feet and ankles (edema), changes in urination frequency, muscle cramps, and persistent itching. Because early stages often lack symptoms, specific blood and urine tests are necessary to check kidney function.

Prevention and management focus on controlling underlying conditions. Keeping blood sugar and blood pressure within target ranges is crucial. Lifestyle changes such as eating a healthy low-sodium diet, exercising, quitting smoking, and maintaining a healthy weight can help slow the progression of the disease.

Urgent medical care is needed if you experience severe symptoms of kidney failure, such as sudden and severe shortness of breath, confusion, extreme fatigue, or an inability to urinate.
